# CHAPTER TWO: LITERATURE REVIEW

## 2.1 Introduction

For this project, the literature review focused on software-related research addressing machine learning for dental image analysis, smartphone-based health applications, and real-time preventive screening systems. A systematic review was conducted, searching indexed platforms such as IEEE Xplore, PubMed, ScienceDirect, SpringerLink, ACM Digital Library, and Google Scholar, using keywords such as "deep learning dental images," "mobile-accessible oral health system," and "machine learning dental screening." Papers containing solid software solutions in comparison with oral health were filtered, and approximately 20–25 relevant papers were reviewed.

Park et al. (2022) designed a deep learning system based on convolutional neural networks to segment intraoral photographs for caries detection, demonstrating the possibility of software-based dental screening. Cai et al. (2024) proposed an oral image segmentation model with high accuracy in detecting calculus, gingivitis, and caries, while Garg et al. (2023) developed lightweight neural networks capable of running on smartphones to detect oral diseases in real time. Other studies noted the use of YOLO-based mobile applications to detect dental caries with accuracy comparable to junior dentists (Thanh, Van Toan, Vo Truong Nhu, and Nguyen, 2022), and systematic reviews revealed that convolutional neural networks offered high diagnostic accuracy when incorporated into user-centered software packages (Ahmed and Lee, 2024).

On the whole, these articles demonstrated that clinical AI applications were available, but a significant gap existed in consumer-grade, smartphone-based preventive screening — a gap that SmartSmile aimed to fill with mobile-accessible ML models, real-time feedback, and user-friendly design.

## 2.2 Historical Background of the Research Topic

Oral health management had been developing tremendously over the past twenty years, shifting from traditional dental check-ups toward digital technologies and machine learning approaches for early and preventive care. In the past, oral health care was primarily based on clinical meetings and professional imaging, including radiographs, which restricted access to people in low-resource environments (World Health Organization, 2024). The initial efforts to assist with preventive oral health using software were mobile health applications aimed at general oral health care guidance, brushing reminders, and dental disease education (Nzabonimana et al., 2024). Although these applications enhanced awareness, they did not analyze the real-time condition of the user's oral state and failed to make use of AI to offer personalized feedback.

As machine learning and image processing developed, scientists began creating programs that could study dental images and determine the condition of dental caries, plaque formation, and gingivitis. Initial models based on convolutional neural networks showed excellent results on professional intraoral photos in clinical settings (Park et al., 2022; Cai et al., 2024). These systems were, however, restricted to controlled environments and required high-quality images, making them unsuitable for ordinary users with smartphones. More recent software solutions considered smartphone-based detection with deep learning models such as YOLO and MobileNet, which could run efficiently on mobile devices to process RGB images and provide real-time preventive screening (Thanh, Van Toan, Vo Truong Nhu, and Nguyen, 2022).

This historical pattern indicated a distinct direction in which oral health software moved — from general education toward AI-diagnostic features — yet most existing applications remained structured for clinical use. Mobile computing, combined with deep learning, currently allowed the implementation of preventive oral health platforms such as SmartSmile, enabling individuals to monitor their own oral health, predict the earliest signs of oral issues, and request timely professional assistance, even in high-resource or daily contexts (Ahmed and Lee, 2024).

## 2.3 Overview of Existing Systems

This section surveyed existing software applications in the field of dental image analysis and preventive oral health screening. The aim was to demonstrate that while software existed that partially addressed dental care issues, the majority of systems failed to resolve the challenge of implementing real-time, accessible, and preventive screening using smartphone pictures. These systems helped to identify the strengths and weaknesses of the current technological environment in which SmartSmile was positioned.

### 2.3.1 AI-Based Mobile-Accessible Dental Screening Systems

A YOLOv5-based AI smartphone application designed to identify dental caries in intraoral images represented one of the most developed systems available. In a study conducted by Adnan et al. (2025), a YOLOv5s model was trained on thousands of annotated intraoral images and deployed to a smartphone application. Compared to junior dentists, the app demonstrated high performance — a precision of 90.7%, a sensitivity of 85.6%, and an F1 score of 88.0% in detecting dental decay (Adnan et al., 2025). This study demonstrated that deep learning could be useful in detecting caries in a mobile setting, but it was not tested in uncontrolled natural settings.

Other studies evaluated YOLOv3 and Faster R-CNN models on smartphones carrying dental images. These studies found that convolutional neural networks were capable of identifying cavitated lesions with reasonable sensitivity and specificity, but that their performance degraded on smaller early lesions (Krothapalli & Cherukumalli Kapalavayi, 2025). This implied that deep learning had potential, but that real-world pictures — with variable lighting, angles, and quality — posed significant challenges to existing software.

### 2.3.2 Usability-Focused Oral Health Applications

A number of commercial and research applications dealt with user perceptions and self-monitoring rather than automatic diagnostics. An AI-driven application called TestMyTeeth was tested and found useful by end-users, but it was unable to consistently obtain clear intraoral images, and interface navigation posed challenges (Al-Zubaidy et al., 2025). These usability issues were not limited to a particular age or education level, demonstrating that software usability remained a significant limitation to successful adoption.

A caries risk self-assessment and preventive education mHealth application called CarieCheck was created with high usability and perceived quality scores (CarieCheck research, 2025). However, these applications aided in education and risk awareness but still failed to incorporate real-time machine learning-based identification of visible signs on user photos, and were therefore not yet useful as actual screening tools.

### 2.3.3 Prototype and Research Tools

Outside commercial systems, research prototypes focused on image analysis with user interaction. OralCam was a mobile system based on deep learning that enabled individuals to self-examine various oral conditions using smartphone images and extra user-centered context information. OralCam demonstrated good detection sensitivity in laboratory testing, but as a research prototype it was not easily accessible as a general-use device, and its implementation required additional evolution to allow general consumer use (Liang et al., 2020).

Similarly, machine learning studies such as that of Garg et al. (2023) created lightweight neural networks optimized for mobile phones that could detect calculus and other characteristics based on RGB oral images, with promise of near real-time analysis, but these were not yet implemented in readily available applications.

## 2.4 Review of Related Work

Recent research on AI-based dental screening and preventive oral health software had grown, mainly due to the need for accessible, early detection tools. Many studies explored using machine learning and mobile health technologies to detect dental caries, plaque, gingivitis, and other oral conditions from images captured either in clinical or home settings.

Much attention had been paid to deep learning in dental image analysis. The AI system presented by Adnan et al. (2025) was based on the YOLOv5 algorithm and was trained on intraoral photographs to identify dental caries. The system achieved an F1-score of 88%, demonstrating that convolutional neural networks were capable of detecting carious lesions. The experiment was, however, conducted under controlled conditions using professionally captured images, which could hinder performance on consumer-grade photographs. Similarly, Krothapalli and Cherukumalli Kapalavayi (2025) reviewed AI models such as Faster R-CNN and MobileNet-based systems, concluding that although AI could effectively detect dental decay with high accuracy, real-world performance could be affected by variations in light, image quality, and user technique.

Other research focused on smartphone-accessible preventive tools. AICaries, a mobile system tested by Al-Jallad et al. (2022), guided parents to detect early childhood caries using phone cameras. The research indicated a high level of usability and user satisfaction, suggesting that smartphones had prospective value in preventing oral health issues. However, the application was restricted to young children and image quality posed a problem with regard to diagnostic accuracy. Similarly, TestMyTeeth, tested by Al-Zubaidy et al. (2025), gave users an opportunity to monitor dental plaque via AI, but many users could not take clear intraoral pictures, and usability scores were not high, indicating difficulty in making the system entirely user-friendly.

Oral health screening potential was also mentioned in several research prototypes with AI driving. Liang et al. (2020) created OralCam, an application that used AI to analyze smartphone pictures and integrate them with user-provided data to determine oral health state. Though OralCam was encouraging in controlled tests, it had yet to be implemented on a large scale. Garg et al. (2023) developed lightweight neural networks optimized for mobile devices in detecting calculus and gingival inflammation. Such models offered near real-time analysis but remained experimental and were not widely accessible to ordinary users.

These research findings indicated that AI and mobile technologies played a crucial role in the oral health screening process, and that image-based identification and greater access were possible. Nonetheless, obstacles such as reliance on high-quality images, limited population coverage, usability issues, and decreased accuracy in uncontrolled settings represented common challenges across the research landscape.

## 2.5 Summary of Reviewed Literature

The literature review showed that artificial intelligence and mobile-accessible health technologies had developed greatly in the field of preventive oral health. Research by Krothapalli and Cherukumalli Kapalavayi (2025) indicated that deep learning models such as YOLOv5 and Faster R-CNN could diagnose dental conditions with high precision when using clinical or controlled images. Mobile applications such as AICaries (Al-Jallad et al., 2022) and TestMyTeeth (Al-Zubaidy et al., 2025) demonstrated that mobile applications could enhance access to preventive screening and increase oral health awareness. Other options, including OralCam (Liang et al., 2020) and lightweight mobile neural network prototypes (Garg et al., 2023), also emphasized that oral health monitoring could be offered through real-time or near real-time image analysis.

In spite of these developments, several severe limitations remained. The majority of systems depended on quality, professionally taken pictures and were therefore less accurate when employed with standard smartphones. Numerous applications were specific to certain groups of people, such as children, and were not generalizable to adults in various situations. Common usability problems included the inability to obtain clear intraoral images, light management, or the appropriate angle for image capture. In addition, the majority of solutions were tested in controlled or laboratory environments, restricting evidence of performance in real-world settings. Not many applications offered personalized and actionable preventive feedback urging users to take necessary measures or visit specialists. Lastly, available tools did not combine various elements of preventive oral health — including education, real-time screening, and advice on secondary care — into one accessible platform.

These gaps showed that though AI and mobile-accessible health applications had demonstrated the viability of image-based detection and preventive awareness, no existing solution could handle the requirement of a trustworthy, conveniently applicable, real-time preventive oral health check system for general users with consumer-grade smartphones. This brought out the need to conduct additional research to develop and test a comprehensive system that could overcome the technical, usability, and accessibility constraints of currently available solutions.

## 2.5 Strengths and Weaknesses of Existing Systems

Existing software solutions in the field of oral health screening and dental image analysis showed promising advances, especially with the use of artificial intelligence and machine learning. Some systems demonstrated strengths in integrating modern computational techniques and improving accessibility to preventive oral care information. However, many of these software systems still faced important limitations that prevented them from fully solving the problem of accessible, accurate, real-time, preventive oral health screening for everyday smartphone users.

An obvious advantage of existing solutions was the use of AI and deep learning models to identify dental conditions in images. Research revealed that deep learning-based AI systems were capable of good performance in the detection of dental caries and other oral health indicators using photographic data and clinical images as input. A systematic review of deep learning-based dental caries detection models stated that deep learning achieved high diagnostic accuracy, with F1-scores and detection rates comparable to traditional diagnostic methods (Vach et al., 2024; Shams et al., 2024). Similarly, AI systems built on MobileNet-v3 and U-Net structures demonstrated generally high accuracy in detecting caries when used in clinical settings (Shams et al., 2024). These findings indicated that AI-oriented tools could be used in practice to aid oral disease detection and be transferred to mobile applications.

The possibility of accessibility through smartphones was another strength. An increasing number of applications, including TestMyTeeth and AICaries, enabled users to take pictures of their mouths at home and receive automated feedback. TestMyTeeth enabled users to track their dental plaque through AI, allowing people to interact with their oral health outside the clinic (Al-Zubaidy et al., 2025). AICaries was found to be a measure of screening early childhood caries, and parents who used the app claimed it was helpful in detecting and educating about risk factors at home (Al-Jallad et al., 2022). These mobile solutions demonstrated that mobile-accessible platforms could deliver preventive oral health devices to the daily lives of users.

In spite of these advantages, apparent weaknesses in current software systems restrained their utility. One of them was usability problems and difficulties in capturing images. Surveys assessing TestMyTeeth results revealed that users had difficulties making clear intraoral images, particularly of difficult-to-access posterior teeth, and a significant number needed technical assistance to operate the application correctly (Al-Zubaidy et al., 2025). The mean System Usability Scale (SUS) value of 56.2 showed that it was not good — only marginally acceptable — which was well below the industry average for good usability (Al-Zubaidy et al., 2025). This indicated that bad user experience and inability to take good images could diminish the trustworthiness of any type of software screening tool.

The second weakness was that the actual usage and testing of AI models in real-life scenarios had little actual performance data, particularly when not in a controlled clinical environment. Systematic reviews demonstrated that AI models tended to be effective when applied to curated data containing high-quality images, and their performance significantly decreased when applied to images captured by average users in uncontrolled conditions, including smartphone photos (Shams et al., 2024; Vach et al., 2024). The sensitivity and specificity metrics also lacked standard values across studies, pointing to the fact that a great number of AI systems could not be used in reality to prevent rather than treat diseases without professional supervision (Shams et al., 2024).

Another weakness related to generalizability and validation. Many AI models applied to dental screening had not been extensively validated across various population groups, types of devices, and imaging parameters. Without such validation, models could be biased toward the data used to train them and not be effective on users whose demographic or environmental factors did not match the training data, particularly in low-resource or underrepresented groups (Vach et al., 2024).

Moreover, most available oral health systems were more educational, remedial, or self-monitoring rather than actual diagnostic screening with rich image analysis. mHealth oral health scoping reviews indicated that although numerous apps facilitated hygiene routines, few included advanced image-based AI diagnostics or remote clinical studies — in other words, they did not detect even the initial signs of illness on the basis of photos (Väyrynen et al., 2023; Al-Zubaidy et al., 2025).

To summarize, existing software for oral health screening showed significant empirical progress in terms of AI integration and mobile accessibility, but lacked adequate usability, real-world functionality, external validation, and advanced diagnostic features. Such constraints implied that current systems were frequently unable to deliver precise, personalized, real-time feedback to common individuals based on smartphone-taken photos. SmartSmile aimed to close these gaps by improving usability, increasing the strength of AI analysis of consumer-level photos, and providing actionable preventive advice to users regardless of their diverse real-world settings.

## 2.6 General Comment and Conclusion

The findings of this literature review provided strong support for the present research study. Mobile-accessible health technologies and artificial intelligence had already been successfully used in the context of preventive oral health screening (Krothapalli & Cherukumalli Kapalavayi, 2025), and the analysis of dental images had been established as a promising area of research with extensive descriptions of future uses in caries, plaque, and other prevalent conditions (Al-Zubaidy et al., 2025; Al-Jallad et al., 2022). This created an obvious reason to create such a system and proved that it was possible to combine AI-driven analysis with the accessibility of the smartphone.

Nevertheless, crucial gaps existed at the intersection of population-specific needs and real-life situations, such as difficulties with consumer-level image capture, the variety of users, usability issues, and missing personalized preventive feedback (Liang et al., 2020; Garg et al., 2023). Future measures therefore aimed at overcoming these limitations by developing a system that would facilitate proper real-time screening, user-friendly instructions toward the capture of clear intraoral images, and practical recommendations that would lead to early referral to professional care. These findings were used to develop specific technical architecture, model development, and functionality strategies detailed in the following chapter on system analysis and design.
